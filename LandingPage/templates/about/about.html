ACCREDITATION: AN OVERVIEW

What is Accreditation?

Accreditation is a concept, a process, and a status that:

  • Acts as a means of self-regulation, focusing on evaluating and continuously improving educational quality.
  • Provides a framework for public accountability, ensuring that educational institutions consistently seek ways to upgrade and enhance the quality of education and training they offer.

Why is Accreditation Important?

Accreditation holds significant importance due to its role in:

  • Ensuring and maintaining educational quality standards.
  • Offering credibility and trust to stakeholders, including students, employers, and the public.
  • Facilitating continuous enhancement and development in educational practices.

Who Benefits from Accreditation?

The beneficiaries of accreditation include:

  • Students and learners pursuing education from accredited institutions.
  • Employers seeking competent and skilled graduates.
  • Communities relying on the quality of professionals trained by accredited institutions.

Features of Accreditation

Key aspects of accreditation include:

  • Focus on evaluation and continuous improvement.
  • Structural framework for public accountability.
  • Commitment to ongoing quality enhancement in education and training.

Difference Between Program and Institutional Accreditation

Program accreditation refers to the evaluation and recognition of specific programs within an institution, while institutional accreditation pertains to the assessment of the entire institution as a whole.

Factors Influencing Accreditation Success

The success of accreditation depends on:

  • Adherence to established quality standards and criteria.
  • Commitment to continuous improvement.
  • Transparency in processes and practices.
  • Engagement of stakeholders in the accreditation process.
